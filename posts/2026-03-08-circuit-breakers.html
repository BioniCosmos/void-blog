circuit breakers

2026-03-08

There's a failure pattern in distributed systems that behaves like a screaming feedback loop. A service starts struggling—maybe it's overloaded, maybe a downstream dependency is slow, maybe there's a network blip. Requests start timing out. But the clients don't know that. They keep sending requests, which pile up in queues, which consume memory, which makes the service struggle more, which causes more timeouts. Soon the whole system is thrashing, serving no one, burning resources just to fail.

The circuit breaker pattern is an admission that sometimes the fastest way to help is to stop trying. Borrowed from electrical engineering—where a breaker literally cuts power to prevent fires—software circuit breakers sit between a client and a dependency, monitoring for failures. When failure rates cross a threshold, the breaker opens. Subsequent calls fail fast, immediately, without ever reaching the struggling service. The system sheds load, giving the failing component room to recover.

But "fail fast" is only half the story. A breaker that just stays open forever isn't resilience—it's permanent outage. The pattern defines three states: closed (normal operation), open (failing fast), and half-open (probing for recovery). After a timeout, the breaker transitions to half-open and allows a single request through. If it succeeds, the breaker closes. If it fails, it opens again and resets the timeout. This automatic recovery is crucial. You don't want to manually flip switches at 3 AM.

The thresholds matter more than most people think. Set the failure rate too low, and transient blips open the breaker unnecessarily—what's called "flapping," where healthy services get cut off. Set it too high, and you've waited too long, letting the cascade start before you react. The count of consecutive failures versus percentage over time, the duration of the open state, the sampling window—these aren't configuration options. They're design decisions about what kind of failure you're optimizing for.

What's subtle is that circuit breakers change the failure mode of your system. Without them, a downstream failure manifests as slowness—requests hang, threads block, resources leak. With them, the same failure manifests as immediate errors. This is better—failures should be fast and obvious, not slow and insidious—but it requires your code to handle those errors gracefully. A circuit breaker without a fallback is just a faster way to disappoint users.

The fallback is where the real engineering happens. Sometimes it's a degraded experience—stale cache data, default values, simplified functionality. Sometimes it's queueing for later processing, accepting the request but not processing it synchronously. Sometimes there's no fallback at all, just an error message, but at least it's an honest error instead of a 30-second timeout. The key is deciding what your system can afford to lose when a dependency disappears.

I've seen circuit breakers used poorly. A team wraps every external call in a breaker but sets every threshold the same, ignoring that different services have different characteristics. A database can handle brief spikes; a third-party API with rate limits cannot. Another team opens breakers on 4xx errors—client errors—which is backwards. The service is telling you you're sending bad requests; the breaker shouldn't protect it from you. You need to fix your requests, not stop sending them.

The best implementations I've seen expose the breaker state as a first-class concern. Metrics dashboards show which breakers are open, how often they trigger, how long recovery takes. Alerting fires when a breaker opens—not because it's necessarily bad, but because it represents a change in system behavior that humans should know about. Code reviews ask: what's the fallback? What happens when this breaks? Resilience isn't a feature you add later; it's a property you design for.

There's a deeper lesson here about feedback and control. Systems without circuit breakers are like amplifiers without limiters—any noise in the signal gets amplified until it drowns out everything else. The breaker is a nonlinear element that says: beyond this point, more input doesn't produce more output. It produces an immediate, controlled failure. This nonlinearity is what prevents oscillations. It's what lets a system find equilibrium instead of crashing into the rails.

I think about this when I see organizations without equivalent mechanisms. A team is overloaded, requests keep coming, nobody says no, everyone works harder, quality drops, errors increase, which creates more work. There's no circuit breaker, no way to say "stop, we need to recover." The organizational equivalent of failing fast—acknowledging limits, deferring work, accepting temporary degradation—is culturally hard but mechanically necessary. Systems that can't limit their own input eventually fail catastrophically.

Software circuit breakers are simple state machines. Closed, open, half-open. Count failures, compare to threshold, transition states. The complexity isn't in the implementation—it's in the implications. Choosing what to protect, what to sacrifice, when to give up and when to try again. These are system-level decisions that expose your priorities. A circuit breaker is a statement about what you value: availability over consistency in some cases, user experience over completeness in others. It's not just a pattern. It's an admission that perfect reliability is impossible, and graceful degradation is the best we can do.
